Chapter One

The Age of Imperialism

1880s – 1914

"The great powers of the world had an appetite — for land, for resources, for pride — and nothing was going to stop them from eating."

By the late 1800s, the world's most powerful nations had decided that power wasn't enough — they wanted more. This hunger had a name: European imperialism — the drive by European nations to colonize and control territories across Africa, Asia, and beyond. At the same time, across the Pacific, Asian imperialism was taking shape too, as Japan began eyeing its neighbors with similar ambition.

Why did they do it? Three engines drove the empire-building machine — economic reasons (colonies meant raw materials, cheap labor, and new markets), political and military reasons (more territory meant more global leverage and naval bases), and racist reasons (a toxic belief that Western civilization had a duty to "civilize" others). Not everyone agreed — anti-imperialism beliefs pushed back hard, arguing that conquest was immoral and contradicted American democratic values.

The man who made the military case loudest was Alfred T. Mahan, a naval strategist who argued that sea power was world power. His ideas fueled a global naval arms race.

America Looks West: Hawaii

America's first target was paradise. Hawaii's economy had become deeply intertwined with American sugar planters and businessmen, who pressured Washington for annexation. Standing in their way was Queen Liliuokalani, Hawaii's proud monarch, who resisted foreign control. She was overthrown in 1893 with American backing, and Sanford B. Dole — yes, of the pineapple family — became the territory's first governor.

Lili was the last queen. Dole got the deal.

The Spanish-American War: Cuba and Beyond

Thousands of miles away, Cuba was boiling. Jose Marti led a revolution against brutal Spanish colonial rule, but Spain sent General Valeriano Weyler — nicknamed "The Butcher" — to crush it with reconcentration camps that horrified the world.

American newspapers saw opportunity. Yellow journalism — sensationalized, emotionally charged reporting designed to sell papers — turned public outrage into war fever. Papers owned by Hearst and Pulitzer ran lurid headlines, including the leaked De Lôme Letter, in which a Spanish diplomat insulted President McKinley.

Then the spark: the U.S. battleship U.S.S. Maine exploded in Havana Harbor on February 15, 1898. 266 Americans died. The cause was likely an accident, but yellow journalism screamed sabotage. "Remember the Maine!" became the rallying cry. President William McKinley, under enormous pressure, asked Congress for a declaration of war.

The War Itself

Commodore George Dewey struck first — not in Cuba, but in the Philippines — destroying the Spanish fleet in Manila Bay in a single morning. Back in the Caribbean, the famous Rough Riders, a volunteer cavalry unit, charged up San Juan Hill in Cuba in a battle that made Theodore Roosevelt a national hero.

Spain collapsed. The Treaty of Paris (1898) ended the war and handed the U.S. an empire: Cuba (nominally independent), Puerto Rico, Guam, and the Philippines.

Managing the New Empire

But empire was complicated. In the Philippines, Emilio Aguinaldo had fought alongside Americans expecting independence — instead, he got American occupation. The Philippine-American War that followed was brutal, killing hundreds of thousands of Filipinos. Puerto Rico received limited self-governance under the Foraker Act. Cuba was kept under U.S. influence through the Platt Amendment, which gave the U.S. the right to intervene in Cuban affairs at will.

In China, the Open Door Policy demanded that all imperial powers allow equal trade access. When Chinese nationalists called the Boxers rose up against foreign influence in the Boxer Rebellion, a multinational force including U.S. troops crushed it.

Open Door = America knocking on China's door. Boxers tried to close it. The door stayed open.

Chapter Two

The Great War

1914 – 1919

"Europe built a room full of explosives over decades. A single assassination lit the match."

By 1914, Europe was a powder keg. Decades of rivalry, arms buildup, and entangling alliances had created a system so tightly wound that one wrong move could detonate everything.

Four forces had been building for decades — militarism (nations glorifying and building massive armies and navies), the Alliance System (secret treaties that meant one country's war became everyone's war), imperialism (competition for colonies creating constant friction), and nationalism (fierce ethnic pride, especially in the Balkans, that made compromise impossible). Two armed camps faced each other: the Allies (Britain, France, Russia) and the Central Powers (Germany, Austria-Hungary, Ottoman Empire).

The Spark

On June 28, 1914, in Sarajevo, a young Serbian nationalist named Gavrilo Princip shot Archduke Franz Ferdinand — heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne — and his wife. Within weeks, the alliance system had pulled all of Europe into war.

Princip had one bullet and accidentally started a world war.

The War Nobody Expected

Everyone thought it would be over by Christmas. It wasn't. The Western Front became a nightmare of trench warfare — miles of muddy, rat-infested trenches where men died by the thousands for yards of ground. New weapons during WWI made it worse: poison gas, machine guns, tanks, and airplanes turned war into industrial slaughter. Medical issues during WWI — infection, shell shock (what we now call PTSD), and disease — killed nearly as many as bullets. The final toll of the war was staggering: approximately 20 million dead.

America Watches — Then Joins

The U.S. initially stayed out. Reasons for American neutrality included a large immigrant population with ties to both sides, a strong isolationist tradition, and President Wilson's belief in staying above the fray.

But neutrality was hard to maintain. Britain's British Blockade strangled German trade. Germany retaliated with U-Boats — submarines — that prowled the Atlantic. In May 1915, a U-Boat sank the British liner Lusitania — 1,198 died, including 128 Americans. Public outrage spiked.

Then in 1917, the Zimmermann Note — a secret German telegram proposing a military alliance with Mexico against the U.S. — was intercepted by British intelligence. Germany also resumed unrestricted submarine warfare. Wilson asked Congress for war. Approved April 6, 1917.

Mobilizing America

The Selective Service Act created a draft — nearly 3 million men were conscripted. U.S. Army Training was rushed but effective. Women and the military found new roles — serving as nurses, telephone operators, and even Navy clerks. African Americans in the Army served in segregated units, fighting for a democracy that denied them rights at home — the seeds of future civil rights demands.

The home front transformed too. The government impact on shipbuilding was enormous — output multiplied to support the war effort. The Convoy System — grouping merchant ships with naval escorts — dramatically reduced U-Boat losses.

Key figures: General John J. Pershing led the American Expeditionary Forces in Europe, insisting on keeping U.S. forces independent rather than folding them into Allied units. Alvin York, a Tennessee sharpshooter and former conscientious objector, became America's greatest war hero — capturing 132 German soldiers almost singlehandedly in a single engagement.

Ending the War, Breaking the Peace

Germany collapsed in November 1918. Wilson arrived in Paris with his Fourteen Points — an idealistic peace plan centered on national self-determination and a League of Nations to prevent future wars.

At the Paris Peace Conference, the Big Four (Wilson of the U.S., Britain's Lloyd George, France's Clemenceau, Italy's Orlando) negotiated the settlement. France and Britain wanted punishment. The resulting Versailles Treaty humiliated Germany: massive reparations, severe military limits, and a "war guilt clause" forcing Germany to accept sole blame for the war.

Wilson got his League — but back home, the U.S. Senate refused to ratify the treaty. The U.S. never joined the League of Nations — a fatal blow to the organization's authority.

Wilson won the war, lost the peace, and had a stroke fighting for a League his own country wouldn't join.

Chapter Three

World War II

1939 – 1945

"The world had tried to ignore the monsters. By 1939, ignoring them was no longer an option."

▶ Video Resource

The humiliation of Versailles and the devastation of the Great Depression had broken Europe's faith in democracy. Into that vacuum stepped three men who would set the world on fire. Adolf Hitler in Germany, Benito Mussolini in Italy, and Japan's militarist leaders all embraced fascism — a hyper-nationalist, authoritarian ideology that glorified the state, the leader, and military power above all else. Together, Germany, Italy, and Japan formed the Axis Powers, cemented by the Tripartite Pact of 1940.

The Axis was an axle — three wheels spinning toward destruction.

The Holocaust Begins

Before the war started, Hitler's regime was systematically stripping Jews of their rights. The Nuremberg Laws (1935) legally defined Jews as non-citizens, banned intermarriage, and excluded them from public life. On Kristallnacht — "Night of Broken Glass," November 9, 1938 — Nazi mobs destroyed thousands of Jewish businesses and synagogues, killing nearly 100 Jews and sending 30,000 to concentration camps.

Jewish refugees desperately sought escape. Reasons why Jewish immigration to the U.S. was difficult included strict immigration quotas, antisemitism in the State Department, and broad public resistance. Most were turned away. The Holocaust ultimately murdered 6 million Jews, along with millions of others — the many groups victimized by the Holocaust included Roma, disabled people, LGBTQ+ individuals, Slavic peoples, and political dissidents.

Nuremberg stripped rights. Kristallnacht shattered glass and hope. The camps came next.

The Road to Pearl Harbor

Japan's militarist leaders' goals centered on dominating Asia and the Pacific. When Japan invaded China and French Indochina, the U.S. response was an oil and steel embargo — strangling Japan's war machine. Japan faced a choice: back down or strike first.

Admiral Yamamoto planned the blow. Hideki Tojo, Japan's Prime Minister, ordered it. On December 7, 1941, Japanese carrier aircraft attacked Pearl Harbor in a devastating surprise assault, sinking or damaging 8 battleships and killing 2,403 Americans. Americans' perception of the Japanese — already shaped by racism and fear — turned to fury overnight. Japanese perception of Americans was that they were soft and unwilling to fight a long war. Both sides fatally misjudged each other.

12/7/41 — the date which will live in infamy. Japan thought America would negotiate. America declared war the next day.

America Mobilizes

The U.S. war machine was stunning in scale. The War Production Board converted civilian factories to military production virtually overnight. The Office of Price Administration controlled inflation through price controls and rationing. The Office of War Information shaped public messaging and propaganda.

At home, rationing of food, gas, and rubber was universal. Americans planted Victory Gardens in backyards and vacant lots to supplement food supplies. Rosie the Riveter became the iconic face of the millions of women who entered the workforce, many serving formally through the Women's Auxiliary Army Corps (WAAC). GIs — ordinary American servicemen — came from every background, and life for American servicemen was a mix of boredom, terror, and extraordinary camaraderie.

Injustice on the Home Front

Not everyone was treated equally. After Pearl Harbor, Executive Order 9066, signed by FDR, forcibly relocated 120,000 Japanese Americans to internment camps in remote areas. Reasons for Japanese American internment included war hysteria, racism, and political pressure — not evidence of disloyalty. The Japanese American experience during internment was one of lost homes, businesses, and dignity.

Yet the 442nd Regiment, composed almost entirely of Japanese Americans, became the most decorated unit in U.S. Army history — fighting for a country that had imprisoned their families.

African Americans also faced profound contradictions during the war. The Double V Campaign — launched by the Pittsburgh Courier newspaper — demanded victory abroad AND victory over racism at home. A. Philip Randolph, a labor and civil rights leader, threatened a march on Washington that forced FDR to desegregate defense industries. The Zoot Suit Riots in Los Angeles (1943) exposed violent racial tensions beneath the war's unity narrative.

442nd fought hardest for the country that trusted them least.

The Pacific Theater

General Douglas MacArthur commanded Allied forces in the Pacific. Admiral Chester Nimitz commanded the naval war. Together they executed one of history's great strategic reversals.

The turning point came at the Battle of Midway (June 1942) — U.S. code-breakers had cracked Japanese communications, allowing Nimitz to ambush the Japanese fleet. Japan lost 4 carriers and never recovered the initiative. From there, the strategy was island hopping — bypassing heavily fortified Japanese islands and capturing strategically important ones to use as airbases to advance toward Japan.

As American forces closed on Japan, resistance intensified. Kamikaze pilots flew suicide missions into American ships. The Battle of Okinawa (1945) was the bloodiest Pacific battle — 12,000 Americans and 110,000 Japanese killed — and it made planners dread the cost of invading Japan itself.

Midway = the turning point. Island hopping = the path. Okinawa = the warning.

The Atomic Bomb

The answer to Okinawa's warning was already being built in secret. The Office of Scientific Research and Development funded cutting-edge wartime science. The Manhattan Project, led by physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer, built the world's first atomic weapons in just three years at Los Alamos, New Mexico.

Reasons for dropping the atomic bomb remain debated: avoiding a ground invasion of Japan estimated to cost hundreds of thousands of Allied lives, forcing Japan's immediate surrender, and — some historians argue — demonstrating American power to the Soviet Union in the emerging postwar order.

August 6, 1945 — "Little Boy" destroyed Hiroshima. August 9, 1945 — "Fat Man" destroyed Nagasaki. Japan surrendered on August 15.

Two bombs. Two cities. Two dates. The war ended. The nuclear age began.

The War's End and Aftermath

At the Yalta Conference (February 1945), FDR, Churchill, and Stalin negotiated the postwar world's shape. Stalin promised free elections in Eastern Europe. He didn't deliver — and the seeds of the Cold War were planted.

Nazi leaders faced justice at the Nuremberg Trials — the first international war crimes tribunal, establishing that following orders was not a defense for crimes against humanity. The Occupation of Japan under MacArthur dismantled Japanese militarism and rebuilt the country as a constitutional democracy.

Yalta divided the world. Nuremberg judged the villains. Occupation rebuilt the enemy.

Chapter Four

The Cold War & Vietnam

1945 – 1975

"The hot war ended. A cold one began — fought not with armies on battlefields, but with spies, proxies, propaganda, and the constant shadow of nuclear annihilation."

The alliance of convenience between the U.S. and USSR died almost before the victory parades ended. Two superpowers with opposite visions for the world now faced each other across a divided Europe — and eventually, the entire globe.

The defining U.S. strategy was containment — don't let communism spread beyond where it already was. The ideological and geopolitical standoff that defined the next four decades was the Cold War — a conflict fought everywhere except directly between the two superpowers. The Soviets extended their influence over Eastern Europe through satellite nations — countries like Poland, Hungary, and Czechoslovakia that were nominally independent but effectively controlled by Moscow.

Containment = a dam against a communist flood. Satellite nations = the USSR's puppet states orbiting their master.

Building the Western Alliance

The Truman Doctrine (1947) declared that the U.S. would support free peoples resisting communist takeover — beginning with Greece and Turkey. The Marshall Plan pumped $13 billion into rebuilding Western Europe's economies, betting that prosperity was the best defense against communist appeal.

When the Soviets blockaded West Berlin in 1948, the U.S. responded with the Berlin Airlift — flying in supplies around the clock for nearly a year, 277,000 flights, until the Soviets backed down. The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) formalized the Western military alliance. The Soviets countered with the Warsaw Pact.

The Red Scare at Home

Americans were terrified of communist infiltration. The Loyalty Review Board screened federal employees for communist ties. The House Committee on Un-American Activities (HUAC) held televised hearings. The Hollywood Ten — filmmakers and writers who refused to cooperate with HUAC — were jailed for contempt of Congress and blacklisted from the entertainment industry.

Alger Hiss, a State Department official, was accused of being a Soviet spy — later convicted of perjury in a case that launched Richard Nixon's career. Ethel and Julius Rosenberg were convicted of passing atomic secrets to the Soviets and executed in 1953.

Senator Joseph McCarthy weaponized the fear. McCarthyism — the practice of making sweeping, evidence-free accusations of communist loyalty — destroyed careers and reputations for years. McCarthy finally overreached in the Army-McCarthy hearings of 1954 and was censured by the Senate.

McCarthy saw communists everywhere. Eventually, everyone saw through McCarthy.

Eisenhower, Brinkmanship, and Covert Action

President Eisenhower embraced brinkmanship — pushing confrontations to the edge of war to force the Soviets to back down, relying on nuclear deterrence rather than expensive conventional armies. The H-Bomb (hydrogen bomb) made the original atomic bomb look small — a thousand times more powerful. Both sides built thousands. The Eisenhower Doctrine extended the Truman Doctrine to the Middle East.

Rather than direct military confrontation, Eisenhower preferred covert action. The CIA engineered Covert Action in Iran (1953, restoring the Shah) and Covert Action in Guatemala (1954, installing a military dictatorship). Neither ended well in the long run.

Nikita Khrushchev took over the Soviet Union after Stalin's death, shocking the world with a "secret speech" denouncing Stalin's crimes — but still crushing the Hungarian uprising of 1956.

Space, Spies, and Summits

The Soviets launched Sputnik in 1957 — the world's first satellite — and America panicked. If they could orbit a satellite, they could deliver a nuclear warhead anywhere on Earth. The space race was on.

Then in 1960, a U-2 spy plane piloted by Francis Gary Powers was shot down over the Soviet Union. The U-2 Incident wrecked a planned superpower summit and deepened Cold War mistrust at a critical moment.

Sputnik started the space race. The U-2 ended détente before it began.

Vietnam: The Slow Descent

Vietnam had been fighting for independence for decades. Ho Chi Minh — nationalist, communist, and relentless — led the Vietminh in defeating France at the climactic Battle of Dien Bien Phu (1954). The Geneva Accords temporarily divided Vietnam at the 17th parallel, with nationwide elections promised to reunify it. The elections never happened.

The U.S. backed Ngo Dinh Diem, the corrupt, unpopular South Vietnamese leader, because the alternative was communist reunification under Ho Chi Minh. The Domino Theory — the belief that if one nation fell to communism, its neighbors would follow like dominoes — made Vietnam feel existential to American policymakers. Southern communist guerrillas, the Vietcong, used the Ho Chi Minh Trail — a supply network running through Laos and Cambodia — to fight the South Vietnamese government.

Ho Chi Minh = the man who beat France, then America, then reunified Vietnam.

America Goes to War

In August 1964, the Tonkin Gulf Resolution gave President Johnson virtually unlimited authority to escalate U.S. involvement — based on disputed, likely fabricated reports of attacks on U.S. ships. The architects of escalation: Robert McNamara (Secretary of Defense, who believed the war was winnable with enough data), Dean Rusk (Secretary of State and Cold War hawk), and General William Westmoreland (U.S. commander, who believed enemy attrition would work).

U.S. strategy was the War of Attrition — kill enough Vietcong to break their will. It failed. Vietcong tactics — guerrilla warfare, booby traps, tunnel networks, blending with civilians — made conventional attrition impossible. The U.S. deployed Agent Orange (a defoliant that destroyed jungle cover and caused lasting health damage) and Napalm (a jellied incendiary that burned everything it touched).

Attrition assumed the Vietcong would run out of fighters. They didn't.

The War Comes Home

Morale of U.S. soldiers in Vietnam deteriorated badly as the war dragged on — drug use, racial tension, and cynicism increased. The draft during Vietnam fell disproportionately on poor and minority men; college deferments protected the wealthy. African Americans in Vietnam served in disproportionate numbers and faced racism both in-country and when they returned home. Women in Vietnam served as nurses and support personnel, often under fire — 265,000 women served.

At home, the Living Room War — the first war televised nightly into American living rooms — eroded public support in ways no previous war had. The Credibility Gap widened as the government's optimistic claims collided with nightly images of body bags and burning villages.

The New Left — a broad movement of young progressive activists — organized against the war. Students for a Democratic Society (SDS) mobilized on campuses nationwide. The Free Speech Movement at Berkeley in 1964 had already established that students would challenge authority. In the political arena, Doves (antiwar) and Hawks (pro-war) fought for public opinion.

Nixon, Withdrawal, and Collapse

Richard Nixon won the 1968 election promising a secret plan to end the war. His strategy was Vietnamization — turning the fighting over to South Vietnamese forces and gradually withdrawing Americans. But Nixon also secretly expanded the war: the Invasion of Cambodia (1970) to destroy Vietcong sanctuaries ignited the largest campus protests in American history. At Kent State, National Guardsmen shot and killed four student protesters. The nation was traumatized.

The Pentagon Papers — a secret Defense Department study leaked by Daniel Ellsberg in 1971 — revealed that multiple administrations had systematically lied to the public about Vietnam. Henry Kissinger negotiated the Paris Peace Accords (1973), ending U.S. involvement. It was less a peace than a "decent interval" before South Vietnam fell anyway.

The My Lai Massacre — in which U.S. soldiers murdered hundreds of unarmed Vietnamese civilians in 1968 — became public in 1969 and horrified the world, raising profound questions about the war's conduct.

Returning American veterans came home to indifference or hostility — no parades, few support systems, lasting trauma. The effects of the Vietnam War on America were profound: shattered public trust in government, the "Vietnam Syndrome" reluctance to use military force abroad, the end of the draft, and a generation scarred by loss and division.

Nixon promised to end the war. He expanded it first, then withdrew. Saigon fell in 1975.
